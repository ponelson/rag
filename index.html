Intro to RAG

RAG is an AI technique that improves the accuracy of generative models by pulling facts from an external knowledge base before generating a response. Think of it as an open-book test for AI — it sharply reduces errors and lets the model answer using up-to-date, specific data instead of whatever it happened to memorize in training.

R Step 1 · Retrieval

Find the facts

When you ask a question, the system searches an external source — company documents, a vector database, the live web — for the relevant material.

A Step 2 · Augmentation

Attach the context

It bundles that retrieved material onto your original prompt, so the model sees the question and the evidence together.

G Step 3 · Generation

Write the answer

The model reads the prompt plus the retrieved facts and crafts a grounded answer — often citing the exact sources it used.

The challenge is that RAG assumes clean, current, retrievable, governed data already exists. It often doesn't. Making data worth retrieving — ingesting, refining, embedding, governing, keeping it fresh — is the heavy, perpetual part RAG skips. Adding the D of Data and keeping the RAG agentic loop On is the full picture: The DRAGOn. 🐉
Why teams use it
  • Up-to-date knowledge — reach real-time or freshly updated data with no expensive retraining.
  • Fewer hallucinations — the model is held to the facts in the reference documents.
  • Private company data — let employees or customers query internal wikis, manuals, and databases securely.
  • Auditable — answers cite their sources, so compliance and legal can trace them back.
Where you see it
  • Support chatbots — reference a product catalog or troubleshooting guide on the fly.
  • Internal enterprise search — ask conversational questions to find files or policies on a local server.
  • Smart search engines — scan multiple live pages, summarize, and link the sources.

The missing layer · the data substrate · the drag
Why the front of the wheel is the battleground

Long context didn't kill it

Million-token windows were supposed to make retrieval obsolete. The opposite happened — retrieval became the heavy filter that whittles petabytes of company data down to the few megabytes worth loading into the window. Bigger memory doesn't fix permissions, freshness, or cost.

The moat moved

The edge in enterprise AI has shifted off the smartest model and the cleverest prompt and onto the cleanest, most accurately indexed, best-governed context layer. That layer is the D — easy to demo, brutal to master.

Garbage in, garbage out

Badly formatted files, conflicting sources, and missing metadata — ownership, expiry, domain tags — break retrieval before the model ever runs. Data quality isn't hygiene; it's the gate the whole pipeline passes through.

Plain-English terms

RAG

A model only knows what it absorbed in training — frozen and generic. Retrieval fetches the relevant pieces of your data at question time and hands them to the model as context. Retrieval is the lookup; generation is the model writing from what was retrieved.

Substrate

The foundation a thing draws from. A RAG substrate is the body of data — plus the structure that makes it findable — that the retrieval step reaches into.

Embeddings

Numerical fingerprints of meaning: a passage or file turned into a list of numbers, so things that mean similar things sit near each other. Expensive derived assets — on an open substrate they persist in open format, so they travel with the data.

Index

A fast-lookup structure built over the embeddings — "find the passages that mean roughly this" — instead of scanning everything. The disposable accelerator, not the asset: engine-specific and rebuildable. Like the index at the back of a book: it holds no chapters, it points you to a page.

Enrichment

Adding derived understanding over time — entities, summaries, classifications, embeddings, provenance, eligibility. Enriching continuously means every analysis pass writes more understanding back, so the asset grows richer instead of staying a dumb blob.

D

Data

Qumulo owns it · the substrate, and the part that's actually heavy

Where the bytes live, how they're laid out, how they stay fresh, how they're refined into something retrievable, and who's allowed to touch them. This is the data substrate — and it's five things, not one.

Where the actual bytes sit and stay put. Every other layer ultimately reads from and writes to here; it's the foundation the rest sit on top of. On top of an S3/file interface you lay open formats so anything can read it.

R

Retrieval

Qumulo owns it · the index and the lookup

The vector and semantic index plus keyword/metadata search and, increasingly, a knowledge-graph layer for connection, exposed through whatever interface a caller speaks. Either it lives where the data lives — or a dedicated vector database gets bolted alongside, and you carry a second system.

R1Semantic / vector index find by meaning▾

Converts content into vectors and retrieves the closest by similarity — pulls up material relevant to a question even when none of the words line up. The library searched before answering. Built over the embeddings; the index itself is the rebuildable accelerator, not the asset.

Who plays here · 2026 leaders
PineconeWeaviateQdrantMilvus / ZillizpgvectorChromaLanceDBAzure AI SearchVespa
Above ~1B vectors, Vespa and Milvus are the production options. LanceDB is the natural fit on an open substrate — vectors-as-files, designed to live directly on object storage.
R2Lexical & metadata search find by exact terms and filters▾

Classic full-text (BM25) and structured metadata filtering — the precision complement to fuzzy semantic search. Names, IDs, codes, dates, eligibility predicates: the places where exact match, not "roughly this," is what you need.

Who plays here
ElasticsearchOpenSearchApache SolrAzure AI SearchVespa
R3Hybrid retrieval fuse meaning + precision▾

Combines vector and keyword results (e.g. reciprocal rank fusion) so you get both semantic recall and lexical precision in one pass. In practice this is the single biggest lever on retrieval quality — which is why every serious engine now ships it.

Who plays here · ships hybrid by default
WeaviateQdrantVespaAzure AI SearchElasticsearch
R4Graph / relationship retrieval find by connection — GraphRAG▾

Vector search is strong on similarity but weak on connection. Layering a knowledge graph over the vectors maps entities and their explicit relationships, so the model can answer cross-document, multi-hop questions — "which compliance issues recurred across every European entity last quarter?" — that pure similarity can't reach. The fastest-moving addition to enterprise retrieval.

R6The retrieval fork bolt-on · captive · open▾

The architectural choice that decides your copies, freshness, lock-in, and operational drag. Remember the index is a disposable accelerator, not the asset — so the real question isn't "who runs the index," it's who holds the durable derived understanding. Three stances answer it differently.

Stance 1 · Bolt-on — a separate system alongside

A dedicated vector DB sits alongside; you sync data into it and run a second system, and the embeddings live in the vendor's proprietary store. Changing engines means re-deriving, not just re-pointing.

Stances 2 & 3 · two ways to be storage-native — and they are not the same claim
⚠ Captive R · the copy's implicit claim
We are the vector store
"No separate database — search and reason in place."

The maximal all-in-one promise: Qumulo replaces the vector DB and the query engine and reasons over the data itself. Cleanest story to tell — and the most roadmap-heavy to back. This is what the marketing implies; it's also the overclaim to watch.

◆ Open R · the durable, defensible position
We hold the asset; we feed the engine

You own D and the durable, open form of the derived understanding — embeddings, entities, provenance in open Parquet/Iceberg, in the customer's custody. You feed the volatile parts of retrieval — the serving index, the query engine — rather than replacing them. Swap engines by re-pointing a new index at data you still hold; never re-derive.

Why Open R is the thesis-consistent one. It's the same line as the whole wheel — own the data (durable, open, owned), feed the intelligence (volatile, fed). The embeddings are data; the index and engine are intelligence. Captive R quietly re-files the index and engine as yours, which is exactly where the claim gets ahead of the product. Lead with Open R; let Captive R be a roadmap aspiration, not a present promise.

On · the move that turns the line into a wheel

On

The agentic close. The model doesn't just answer — it acts: calls tools, triggers workflows, writes results back. That write-back lands as new Data at D, gets re-refined and re-indexed, and the loop runs again. Not D→R→A→G and done — it stays on, turning continuously. That's the whole "no staleness, no manual re-indexing" line made literal: the wheel stays on.

D→R→A→G→On→new data↺D
Not stages — planes that wrap the whole wheel
The bearing

Memory

The state carried across turns of the loop — conversation, scratchpad, long-term store. It doesn't sit at one letter; it's what lets the wheel keep its place as it turns.

The brake

Governance

Guardrails, audit, and eval over everything — every letter, every turn. Identity-level access lives down in D5; this is the policy and oversight layer that rides on top of all of it.

The synthesis worth keeping

Qumulo owns every part of DRAGON that is data — and feeds every part that is intelligence.

Owns — the data

The heavy, recurring, unglamorous front of the wheel that everyone else treats as solved — and the direction the market is consolidating toward: one governed substrate, the golden source of truth every agent reads from.

  • D — Data. Storage, formats, replication, refining, governance: the whole data substrate.
  • R — Retrieval (Open R). Own the durable embeddings, entities & provenance in open format; feed the volatile index and engine.
  • On — the landing. Write-back is just new data arriving at D; the loop stays on.

Feeds — the intelligence

Left to partners, reached through clean interfaces, never owned.

  • A — Augmentation. Orchestrators, SDKs, rerankers, managed platforms.
  • G — Generation. Models, runtimes, silicon, the venues they run on.
  • The model itself. Consumes the stack; gets its own home above it.

Keep the discipline: "owns the data" is the right strategic claim, but the maximal Captive R promise — "we are the vector store, search and reason in place" — is the part most ahead of shipping product. The Open R framing (own the durable derived assets in open format; feed the volatile index and engine) is defensible today; D4 enrichment is closer to thesis than ship. Distinguish what retrieves over data in place today from what the roadmap promises before any of this becomes a customer commitment.
