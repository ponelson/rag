A field guide to the wheel under retrieval

DRAG

RAG is the easy three letters. The D is the drag.

RAG's R quietly assumes a clean, current, retrievable, governed corpus already exists. It doesn't. Making data worth retrieving — ingesting, refining, embedding, governing, keeping it fresh — is the heavy, perpetual part the acronym skips. Prepending the D is just honest. And once you add it, the line bends into a wheel.

Every RAG platform retrieves what it's given. None of them make the data worth retrieving. That whole Data stack is the precondition the acronym skips — and it's five things, not one, which is the whole reason it deserves its own letter.

The missing layer · the Refinery · the drag
Plain-English terms

RAG

A model only knows what it absorbed in training — frozen and generic. Retrieval fetches the relevant pieces of your data at question time and hands them to the model as context. Retrieval is the lookup; generation is the model writing from what was retrieved.

Substrate

The foundation a thing draws from. A RAG substrate is the body of data — plus the structure that makes it findable — that the retrieval step reaches into.

Embeddings

Numerical fingerprints of meaning: a passage or file turned into a list of numbers, so things that mean similar things sit near each other. Expensive derived assets — in the Refinery they persist in open format, so they travel with the data.

Index

A fast-lookup structure built over the embeddings — "find the passages that mean roughly this" — instead of scanning everything. The disposable accelerator, not the asset: engine-specific and rebuildable. Like the index at the back of a book: it holds no chapters, it points you to a page.

Enrichment

Adding derived understanding over time — entities, summaries, classifications, embeddings, provenance, eligibility. Enriching continuously means every analysis pass writes more understanding back, so the asset grows richer instead of staying a dumb blob.

R

Retrieval

Qumulo owns it · the index and the lookup

The vector and semantic index plus keyword/metadata search, exposed through whatever interface a caller speaks. Either it lives where the data lives — or a dedicated vector database gets bolted alongside, and you carry a second system.

R1Semantic / vector index find by meaning▾

Converts content into vectors and retrieves the closest by similarity — pulls up material relevant to a question even when none of the words line up. The library searched before answering. Built over the embeddings; the index itself is the rebuildable accelerator, not the asset.

Who plays here · 2026 leaders
PineconeWeaviateQdrantMilvus / ZillizpgvectorChromaLanceDBAzure AI SearchVespa
Above ~1B vectors, Vespa and Milvus are the production options. LanceDB is the natural fit on an open substrate — vectors-as-files, designed to live directly on object storage.
R2Lexical & metadata search find by exact terms and filters▾

Classic full-text (BM25) and structured metadata filtering — the precision complement to fuzzy semantic search. Names, IDs, codes, dates, eligibility predicates: the places where exact match, not "roughly this," is what you need.

Who plays here
ElasticsearchOpenSearchApache SolrAzure AI SearchVespa
R3Hybrid retrieval fuse meaning + precision▾

Combines vector and keyword results (e.g. reciprocal rank fusion) so you get both semantic recall and lexical precision in one pass. In practice this is the single biggest lever on retrieval quality — which is why every serious engine now ships it.

Exposes retrieval through whatever the caller speaks — a vector API, SQL, REST, and increasingly MCP — so any agent or app can reach it without a bespoke integration. The interface is where storage-native retrieval earns its keep: same data, many front doors, no copy.

R5The retrieval fork bolt-on · captive · open▾

The architectural choice that decides your copies, freshness, lock-in, and operational drag. Remember the index is a disposable accelerator, not the asset — so the real question isn't "who runs the index," it's who holds the durable derived understanding. Three stances answer it differently.

Stance 1 · Bolt-on — a separate system alongside

A dedicated vector DB sits alongside; you sync data into it and run a second system, and the embeddings live in the vendor's proprietary store. Changing engines means re-deriving, not just re-pointing.

⚠ Captive R · the copy's implicit claim
We are the vector store
"No separate database — search and reason in place."

The maximal all-in-one promise: Qumulo replaces the vector DB and the query engine and reasons over the data itself. Cleanest story to tell — and the most roadmap-heavy to back. This is what the marketing implies; it's also the overclaim to watch.

◆ Open R · the durable, defensible position
We hold the asset; we feed the engine

You own D and the durable, open form of the derived understanding — embeddings, entities, provenance in open Parquet/Iceberg, in the customer's custody. You feed the volatile parts of retrieval — the serving index, the query engine — rather than replacing them. Swap engines by re-pointing a new index at data you still hold; never re-derive.

Why Open R is the thesis-consistent one. It's the same line as the whole wheel — own the data (durable, open, owned), feed the intelligence (volatile, fed). The embeddings are data; the index and engine are intelligence. Captive R quietly re-files the index and engine as yours, which is exactly where the claim gets ahead of the product. Lead with Open R; let Captive R be a roadmap aspiration, not a present promise.

Act

The model doesn't just answer — it calls tools, triggers workflows, and writes results back. And that write-back lands as new Data at D, gets re-refined and re-indexed, and the loop runs again. Not D→R→A→G and done. It turns continuously — which is the whole "no staleness, no manual re-indexing" line made literal.

D→R→A→G→Act→new data↺D
Not stages — planes that wrap the whole wheel
The bearing

Memory

The state carried across turns of the loop — conversation, scratchpad, long-term store. It doesn't sit at one letter; it's what lets the wheel keep its place as it turns.

The brake

Governance

Guardrails, audit, and eval over everything — every letter, every turn. Identity-level access lives down in D5; this is the policy and oversight layer that rides on top of all of it.

The synthesis worth keeping

Qumulo owns every part of DRAG that is data — and feeds every part that is intelligence.

Owns — the data

The heavy, recurring, unglamorous front of the wheel that everyone else treats as solved.

  • D — Data. Storage, formats, replication, refining, governance: the Refinery.
  • R — Retrieval (Open R). Own the durable embeddings, entities & provenance in open format; feed the volatile index and engine.
  • Act's landing. Write-back is just new data arriving at D.

Feeds — the intelligence

Left to partners, reached through clean interfaces, never owned.

  • A — Augmentation. Orchestrators, SDKs, rerankers, managed platforms.
  • G — Generation. Models, runtimes, silicon, the venues they run on.
  • The model itself. Consumes the stack; gets its own home above it.

Keep the discipline: "owns the data" is the right strategic claim, but the maximal Captive R promise — "we are the vector store, search and reason in place" — is the part most ahead of shipping product. The Open R framing (own the durable derived assets in open format; feed the volatile index and engine) is defensible today; D4 enrichment is closer to thesis than ship. Distinguish what retrieves over data in place today from what the roadmap promises before any of this becomes a customer commitment.
